​1500-1800


​​Music therapy, the use of music to promote healing and well-being, has a rich history that extends back centuries. Although the formal establishment of music therapy as a profession occurred in the 20th century, there is evidence of therapeutic applications of music throughout different historical periods. This includes the years 1500 to 1800, a time marked by significant developments in music and medicine.

​​The XIX century


​Music therapy, as we know it today, emerged as a distinct discipline in the 20th century. However, the roots of using music for therapeutic purposes can be traced back to the 19th century. During this time, there were several notable developments in the field of music therapy, laying the foundation for its future growth and recognition.

​​First half of XX Century


​In the first half of the 20th century, music therapy continued to evolve and gain recognition as a distinct discipline. Several significant advancements and influential figures contributed to its development during this period.


​The Journal of Music therapy

​The Journal of Music Therapy seeks to advance research, theory, and practice in music therapy through the dissemination of scholarly work. Its mission is to promote scholarly activity in music therapy and to foster the development and understanding of music therapy and music-based interventions. To this end, the journal publishes all types of research, including quantitative, qualitative, historical, philosophical, theoretical, and musical, and may include discipline, profession, and foundational research topics. The journal strives to present a variety of research approaches and topics, to promote critical inquiry, and to serve as a resource and forum for researchers, educators, and clinicians in music therapy and related professions.

Epistemus

Epistemus is a journal that focuses on general musical knowledge, with an emphasis on the study of musical experience. It aims to be a platform for raising and debating issues from a multidisciplinary and international perspective. It particularly addresses cognitive and cultural approaches, without disregarding any paradigm or epistemological perspective with broad recognition in the academic community. The journal publishes original and unpublished works of inquiry, both empirical and theoretical, book and/or scientific event reviews, interviews, pedagogical experiences, and translations that delve into the processes related to the musical experience in its multiple modalities of performance, listening, composition, analysis, teaching, and interpretation, among others.

Page 2

ECOS

ECOS is a refereed scientific journal with a continuous frequency, edited by the Music Therapy Free Chair of the National University of La Plata, Argentina. Its objective is to disseminate academic productions from both Argentina and Latin America and provide a space for the collective construction of knowledge about Music Therapy and related disciplines. The journal represents the opening of a specific space for the visibility of Latin American production and aims to become a source of consultation within the scientific-academic community. ECOS is aimed at an audience composed of researchers, teachers, and professionals in Music Therapy, Psychology, Arts, and other sociocultural disciplines, as well as students from different formations related to the central themes addressed by the publication.
